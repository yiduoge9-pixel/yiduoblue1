Project Statement

About this project

This project explores how short digital messages lose emotional and cultural context across platforms, relationships and languages.

Users input a message, choose a context, reveal a possible misreading, repair the meaning and receive a final communication receipt.

The score is not scientific. It is a design device that makes invisible communication gaps easier to see.

The Chinese-English and Chinese-Cantonese language shifts are also design simulations, not a live translation engine. They are rule-based templates written by hand, built to demonstrate how tone and warmth can change across languages - not to translate arbitrary input accurately.

Portfolio framing: Interactive communication / multilingual misreading / creative coding / user-centred revision.
Portfolio Case Study

The Miscommunication Machine

A creative communication prototype that uses interaction design to make hidden tone, cultural context and digital misreading visible, testable and repairable.

Project Question

How can interaction design reveal the gap between what a sender means and what a receiver reads?

My Role

Concept, UX flow, interface design, HTML/CSS/JS, interaction rules and multilingual writing.

Outcome

A live prototype where users write, misread, compare, repair and download a communication receipt.

Research Insight

Short messages are judged through timing, relationship, platform and cultural expectation, not only through words.

Hong Kong Layer

The Cantonese path explores how written Chinese, Mandarin-influenced phrasing and local conversational rhythm can change emotional meaning.

Communication Aim

The interface turns miscommunication from an invisible feeling into a visible sequence of signals, assumptions and repairs.

Message → Context → Intention → Misread → Compare → Repair → Receipt.

Dynamic rendering, state management, localStorage, modal accessibility and downloadable output.

Design Process

  1. Concept: I started from the phrase “I'm fine” and explored how short messages force readers to guess emotional intention.
  2. Interaction: I designed an 8-step journey from message input to context, intention, misreading, repair and receipt.
  3. Multilingual Layer: I added Chinese-English and Chinese-Cantonese paths to show how tone, rhythm and cultural meaning shift across languages.
  4. Theoretical Framing: My tone categories (polite, indirect, formal, honest, anxious, tired) draw loosely on Edward Hall's distinction between high-context and low-context communication, and on politeness / face-work theory - the idea that speakers manage how much they risk the listener's "face" when they choose directness over indirectness. I used these as a starting lens for grouping tones, not as a strict classification system.
  5. Methodology & Honesty: The score system and the language-transform panel are hand-written design simulations, not a real NLP or translation model. I chose this deliberately so the logic stays fully authored and inspectable, but it means the tool demonstrates a communication idea rather than performing accurate real-world translation.

Testing Protocol

5 target bilingual users
3 feedback questions
Receipt final artifact

The testing stage asks bilingual users to complete the friend conversation and Chinese-Cantonese paths, then identify which step makes the communication gap most visible. The three feedback questions are: (1) which step changed your reading of the message the most, (2) did the repaired version feel natural in your own voice, and (3) where did the tool feel wrong or too generic.

Interview-Ready Pilot Plan

Before presenting this project, I would run a small pilot with 1-2 bilingual friends, watch where they hesitate, and record one direct quote about the friend-conversation path and one direct quote about the Cantonese path.

The most important evidence to collect is whether users trust the repaired sentence, or whether it feels too generic for their own speaking style.

What I Learned

I learned that creative communication is not only about sending a clear message. It is also about designing situations where meaning can be questioned, repaired and reinterpreted.

If I rebuilt this project, I would run bilingual interviews before fixing the tone categories, instead of designing the categories first and testing afterwards. The classification would likely be less like a design taxonomy and closer to how people actually describe misreadings in their own words. I would also be more careful to separate "this is a designed simulation" from "this is measured data" earlier in the experience, not only in the About panel.
